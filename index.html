Overview

By early 2015, more than 81% of the world’s leaders had an active presence in social media. Today, around 3 billion people around the globe use social media — that’s 40% of the world’s population. Social media platforms, advertisers, and increasingly, politicians and governments, use social media to track the behaviour of people and to influence them, particularly during significant events like elections and referenda. Indeed, Freedom House recently reported that online manipulation and disinformation — faciliated by bots and paid trolls — played an “important role in elections in at least 18 countries over the past year, including the United States.”

In the next sessions, we will take a brief look at a number of tools that you can use to retrieve social media data and how to use it to answer certain questions. We will explore the role of social media in the countries in which you work, and discuss what social media can tell us about the quality of an election. We will also look at a practical example of social media monitoring during the recent German parliamentary elections, and discuss the methodological challenges associated with social media data.

Session Objectives

By the end of our sessions, you will:

  • Be familiar with the tools that you can use to retrieve social media data
  • Understand how social media can be used to disseminate disinformation and influence elections
  • Identify key questions that we can answer using social media data
  • Know the challenges associated with making inferences using social media data

About the facilitator

Ray Serrato is a Governance and Innovation Expert at Democracy Reporting International, where he currently manages DRI’s work on technology and democracy and publishes on social media and elections, constitution-making, and transparency and accountability. He has six years of experience working on electoral and constitutional reform projects in Tunisia, Pakistan, Sri Lanka, and Myanmar. Ray previously worked at The Carter Center, where he supported the launch of the Election Obligations and Standards Database and researched election observation and electoral integrity. Prior to his work in democracy assistance, he worked for six years as a communications specialist in the US Armed Forces.
